The COVID-19 pandemic was confirmed to have reached the U.S. state of Wyoming in March 2020. On April 13, Wyoming became the last state in the U.S. to report its first death from COVID-19.[2] On April 14, Wyoming reported its 2nd COVID-19 related death.[3] As of May 14, Wyoming was the state with the fewest number of deaths from COVID-19.[4]

March 2020[edit]

  • March 11: First reported case of COVID-19 is a woman in Sheridan County who recently travelled domestically.[5][6]
  • March 13: Second reported case is a man in Fremont County.[7]
  • March 15: Governor Gordon recommends closing all public schools.[8]
  • March 18: Third positive case in Cheyenne, Wyoming, brings the state count up to 16 confirmed cases.[9]
  • March 24: Yellowstone National Park closes to visitors.[10]

April 2020[edit]

  • April 2: Wyoming Department of Public Health announced it was limiting testing to only six categories.[11]
  • April 17: Democratic Party of Wyoming announced they would conduct their caucus through the mail.[12]
  • April 20: 100 protestors confront Governor Mark Gordon in Cheyenne calling for a reopening of the state.[13]
  • April 23: Expanded testing was announced.[14]
  • April 28: Restaurants, bars, gyms and movie theaters remained closed.[15]

May 2020[edit]

  • May 13: A new statewide order issued allowing K-12 schools to have onsite instruction.[16]
  • May 14:
    • 688 cases confirmed with 7 deaths.[17][18] The University of Wyoming announced the school was moving towards on-campus instruction for the fall semester.[19] It requested $46 million of federal stimulus money to assist with the re-opening on campus.[20]
  • May 15: Restaurants and bars re-open with employees required to wear coverings over their face.[21]
  • May 18:
    • Yellowstone reopened to tourism.[22] The East entrance in Wyoming opened while the West entrance in Montana remained closed.[23] Cars lined up as early as 5:30am to enter the park.[24] Two more deaths were reported that day.[25]
  • May 19:
    • Four nurses at the Wyoming Medical Center, the largest hospital in the state, tested positive for COVID-19.[26] Although health officers warned of an increase in cases, there were no new state restrictions announced[27]
  • May 20: A child care center in Casper closed after one child tested positive for COVID-19.[28]

Governor's Task Force[edit]

On March 17, Wyoming Governor Mark Gordon "announced the creation of five task forces designed to tackle the effects of the spreading coronavirus", and condemned hoarding in the state.[29]

Response from business community[edit]

Industries have had varying responses, with energy companies in the state trying to balance safety with continued productivity by imposing quarantine and travel restrictions.[30] In April and May 2020 the Wyoming Business Council hosted a series of webinars for business owners to help them transition to re-opening their businesses.[31]

Impact on colleges[edit]

As a result of COVID-19, the University of Wyoming decided to offer their courses for the summer of 2020 online.[32] In Mid-May the University announced they were working towards re-opening the campus for the fall 2020 semester.[33] Community colleges announced a variety of closures, including a move to distance learning at Casper College, Central Wyoming College and Northwest College.[34]

Impact on Northern Arapaho tribe[edit]

It was announced on April 21, 2020 that four members of the Northern Arapaho tribe had died from COVID-19.[35] All four had lived in Fremont County.[36] Tribe Chairman Lee Spoonhunter was quoted as saying, "These tribal members were our family members who were dearly loved."[37] Advocacy groups raised concerns COVID-19 could be having a disproportionate impact on Native American tribes as a result of higher rates of preexisting conditions such as diabetes.[38] Some tribal members noted the crisis has also given community members the opportunity to share more oral stories.[39] Similar to the national trend throughout the United States, people of color in the state of Wyoming were disproportionally impacted by COVID-19.[40] As of May 18, 2020, 2.7 percent of COVID-19 cases were American Indian/Alaska Native.[41] Six tribe members had died of COVID-19 as of May 20th.[42] Representative Andi Clifford, a citizen of the Norther Arapaho tribe, made an announcement about the 6th death on facebook.[42]

COVID-19 also had a major impact on the economy of the Northern Arapaho tribe as the casino on the Wind River Indian Reservation was converted into a location for quarantine run by Dr. Paul Ebbert.[43][44]

Impact on sports[edit]

In college sports, the National Collegiate Athletic Association cancelled all winter and spring tournaments, most notably the Division I men's and women's basketball tournaments, affecting colleges and universities statewide.[45] On March 16, the National Junior College Athletic Association also canceled the remainder of the winter seasons as well as the spring seasons.[46] High school sports were also cancelled.[6]

Student athletes at the University of Wyoming were scheduled to return to the Laramie campus on June 1, 2020.[47] A two week quarantine is required.[47]

COVID-19 in Yellowstone[edit]

The National Park Service has issued regular updates to closures in Yellowstone National Park based on CDC guidance.[48] The Thermus aquaticus bacteria found in the termal lakes in Yellowstone has been reported to be useful in testing for COVID-19. Controlling COVID-19 in Yellowstone has been logistically complicated as the national park spans three different states.[33]


Yellowstone reopened to the public with a long line of cars at the East entrance on May 18, 2020 after remaining closed for 7 weeks.[22] Within two hours of opening the park saw 200 cars come through. Only the Wyoming entrance opened initially to control the flow of tourism into the park.[49] Upon opening the park did not allow lodging, food service or any tour buses and many visitors were not wearing masks.[50][49]

Protests[edit]

Throughout April and May there were several protests in Wyoming against the government's orders closing the state. One small protest took place in April in Casper.[51]
